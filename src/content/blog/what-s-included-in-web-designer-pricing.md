## What's Included in Web Designer Pricing? (And What Usually Isn't)

Getting a quote from a web designer and not knowing what's actually covered is one of the most frustrating parts of the hiring process. One designer quotes $800 and another quotes $2,500 — and you have no idea if they're quoting the same thing.

Here's what should be in a typical web design quote, what's commonly left out, and what questions to ask before you sign anything.

### What's Typically Included in a Standard Quote

**Design and layout**  
The visual design of your website — how it looks, how it's structured, where elements sit on the page. This is the core of what a web designer does.

**Development / Build**  
Turning the design into a functioning website. This involves building pages, setting up navigation, making sure everything is connected and works properly.

**Mobile responsiveness**  
Your site should work and look good on every screen size — desktop, tablet, and mobile. Any professional designer should include this as standard. If someone charges extra for mobile optimization in 2024, that's a red flag.

**Basic on-page SEO**  
At a minimum: properly set page titles, meta descriptions, header tag structure, image alt text, and a clean URL structure. This is foundational — your site needs it to have any chance of being found on Google.

**Contact form setup**  
A functional contact or inquiry form connected to your email. Standard for any business site.

**SSL certificate setup**  
That little padlock in the browser bar. It makes your site secure (https://) and is required by Google to avoid ranking penalties. Should be standard.

**Basic analytics integration**  
Connecting Google Analytics (or equivalent) so you can see who's visiting your site. Most professional designers include this.

**Testing and quality checks**  
Before launch, a competent designer checks every page on multiple browsers and devices, confirms all links work, and ensures forms submit correctly.

### What's Often NOT Included (But Might Be Expected)

**Copywriting**  
Writing the actual words on your website is frequently a separate service. If you don't provide content, many designers will charge extra for copywriting — or use placeholder text and leave it to you. Clarify this upfront.

**Photography and imagery**  
Custom photography is almost always separate. Stock photos may be included or may require a licensing fee. Ask.

**Logo design**  
If you don't have a logo, creating one is typically a separate project. Some designers offer it as an add-on.

**Domain name registration**  
Your web address (yourcompany.ca) is usually purchased separately through a registrar like GoDaddy or Google Domains. Some agencies include this; most don't.

**Hosting**  
Where your site lives on the internet. Some agencies include hosting as part of a monthly plan; others build your site and hand it over for you to host yourself. Both are legitimate — just make sure you know which model you're in.

**Email setup**  
Setting up professional email addresses (hello@yourcompany.ca) is often separate. Ask if this is something you need.

**Ongoing maintenance**  
Updates, security patches, backups, and content changes after launch are typically not included in a one-time build price. Many agencies offer monthly maintenance plans.

**Blog setup and population**  
A blog section can be included in the build, but writing and publishing blog posts is almost always extra.

**E-commerce functionality**  
Product pages, shopping cart, payment processing, inventory management — if you need to sell online, expect significant additions to the base price.

### The Questions to Ask Before You Sign

Get clear answers to these before any money changes hands:

- "Does your quote include copywriting, or do I need to provide content?"
- "Is hosting included, or is that separate?"
- "How many pages are covered in this price?"
- "How many rounds of revisions are included?"
- "What happens after launch — is support included?"
- "What would cause the price to go up from this quote?"

A professional designer will answer all of these directly. Vague answers or "it depends" without further explanation is a signal to push harder — or look elsewhere.
