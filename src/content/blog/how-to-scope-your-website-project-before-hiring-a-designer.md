## How to Scope Your Website Project Before Hiring a Designer

The single most important thing you can do before hiring a web designer isn't finding the right platform, comparing portfolios, or even setting a budget. It's getting clear on exactly what you need built.

Vague projects produce vague results — and cost more. A well-scoped brief saves time, prevents scope creep, produces more accurate quotes, and dramatically increases your odds of walking away happy.

Here's how to scope your website project properly.

### Step 1: Define the Purpose of the Site

Start with one clear question: **What is this website supposed to do?**

Every website serves at least one primary business goal. Be specific:

- Generate leads (phone calls, contact form submissions, bookings)
- Sell products directly (e-commerce)
- Build credibility (for referral-based businesses where clients check you out before committing)
- Capture email addresses (for a launch, course, or newsletter)
- Provide information (service details, hours, location) and reduce inbound phone calls

You may have more than one goal, but identify which one is primary. That decision shapes everything — the structure, the copy, the calls to action, the design priority.

### Step 2: Define Your Audience

Who is the website for? The more specifically you can describe your ideal customer, the better your designer can build a site that speaks to them.

Consider:
- Age range and comfort with technology
- Geographic location (local, provincial, national)
- What problem they're trying to solve when they land on your site
- What objections they likely have before buying or inquiring
- Where they're coming from (Google search, referral, social media, direct)

A website built for a 55-year-old contractor looking for a local electrician is structured very differently than one built for a 28-year-old startup founder shopping for SaaS tools.

### Step 3: Identify the Pages You Need

List out every page your site needs at launch. Be realistic — include what's genuinely necessary, not everything you might eventually want. A typical small business site includes:

- Home
- About
- Services (one page, or one page per service if you have several)
- Testimonials / Social proof
- Contact
- FAQ (optional but often useful)
- Blog (optional at launch — can be added later)

If you're unsure whether a page is necessary, ask: "Would a customer need this information to make a decision?" If yes, include it. If no, defer it.

### Step 4: List the Features and Functionality Required

Beyond pages, identify any specific functionality:

- Contact form (standard)
- Booking or appointment system
- E-commerce (product catalogue, cart, checkout)
- Live chat widget
- Email newsletter signup
- Password-protected member area
- Map or location integration
- Social media feeds
- Multi-language support

Be honest about what you actually need at launch vs. what sounds nice. Every feature adds time, complexity, and cost.

### Step 5: Identify Your Content Situation

One of the most common project-stalling issues is content not being ready when the designer needs it. Get honest with yourself:

- Do you have a logo, or does one need to be created?
- Do you have professional photography, or will you use stock images?
- Will you write the page copy, or do you need copywriting as part of the project?
- Do you have testimonials or case studies ready to include?
- Do you have your brand colours and fonts defined?

The more content you have ready at project kick-off, the faster and smoother the build.

### Step 6: Define Your Timeline

When do you need the site live? Work backward from that date and be realistic. A professional small business site takes 2–4 weeks from kick-off to launch. If you need it live in two weeks, communicate that upfront — some designers can accommodate tight timelines, others can't.

Also identify: are there specific events, launches, or marketing campaigns the site needs to be ready for? This context helps your designer prioritize.

### Step 7: Document It All in a Brief

Pull everything above into a simple document — a project brief — before you start talking to designers. It doesn't need to be formal or polished. Even a one-page bulleted summary is enormously helpful.

A basic brief includes:
- Business overview (2–3 sentences)
- Site purpose and primary goal
- Target audience
- List of required pages
- List of required features
- Content status (what you have, what you need)
- Budget range
- Launch timeline

When you share this with designers, you'll get more accurate quotes, faster responses, and a much clearer sense of whether they understand your project.
