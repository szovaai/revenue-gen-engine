It's one of the most common questions in web design — and one of the most inconsistently answered. "Two weeks!" says one designer. "Three to four months," says another. What's actually realistic?

The truth is: it depends on the complexity of the project, the responsiveness of both parties, and how clear the brief is from the start. Here's what typical timelines actually look like.

## Small Business Website (5–10 Pages): 2–4 Weeks

This is the most common scenario — a professional small business site with a home page, about page, services, contact, and maybe a blog or testimonials section.

In a smooth project with a clear brief and responsive communication:

- **Week 1:** Discovery call, scope confirmation, content and asset collection
- **Week 2:** Design mock-up delivered for review
- **Week 3:** Revisions applied, development completed
- **Week 4:** Final review, pre-launch checks, go live

That's a tight but realistic timeline when both sides are organized and responsive.

## What Causes Delays

Most timeline overruns happen on the client side, not the designer's. The most common causes:

- **Slow content delivery.** If you haven't written your page copy or rounded up your photos by the time design starts, the project pauses. This alone can add 2–4 weeks.
- **Unclear decision-making.** If approvals need to go through multiple stakeholders or a committee, revisions take longer.
- **Scope creep.** "Can we add a booking system?" mid-project adds time. Nail down your scope before the project starts.
- **Back-and-forth on revisions.** One or two rounds of revisions is normal. Five rounds suggests the initial brief wasn't clear.

## Larger or More Complex Sites: 6–12 Weeks

If your site includes:

- E-commerce (product catalogue, checkout, inventory)
- A custom booking or reservation system
- Member portals or logins
- Large volumes of content (50+ pages)
- Custom integrations with CRMs, ERPs, or third-party tools

...expect 6–12 weeks minimum.

These projects require more planning, more development time, and more testing before launch.

## "Rush" Jobs: Proceed With Caution

Some designers offer 24–72 hour turnarounds. Occasionally this is legitimate for a very simple landing page. More often, it means a template with minimal customization — which may or may not serve your needs.

A truly professional build simply cannot be rushed to 48 hours. The planning, design, build, and quality checking all take real time. If speed is the most important factor, be honest with yourself about what you're trading off.

## How to Keep Your Project on Schedule

Your single biggest lever is being organized at the start. Before your project begins:

- Write (or at least rough-draft) all your page content
- Gather your logo in high-resolution format
- Round up any photos you want to use
- Have a clear list of the pages you need
- Know your brand colours and any visual preferences
- Have your domain and existing hosting login details ready

The more prepared you are at kickoff, the smoother and faster the project runs.

## What to Ask About Timeline

Before hiring any designer, ask:

- "What's a realistic timeline for my project?"
- "What from me could cause delays?"
- "What happens if we go over the timeline?"

A professional will give you a realistic answer — not just the number they think you want to hear.
