## How to Find a Web Designer Who Understands My Industry

A web designer who has built five sites for businesses like yours brings something genuinely valuable: they've already made the mistakes, learned what works, and built an intuition for what your customers expect. That experience isn't decorative — it saves time, reduces revisions, and often produces a better outcome.

Here's how to find that designer.

### Why Industry Experience Matters

A designer who understands your industry will:

- Know what information your customers need before making a decision
- Understand the trust signals specific to your sector (certifications, licensing, before-and-after work, case studies)
- Already have a sense of what converts in your space
- Know which competitors have strong sites and what makes them strong
- Ask smarter questions in the discovery phase

A generalist can do good work in any industry — but they'll spend more time learning what the specialist already knows.

### How to Search for Industry-Specific Experience

**In Google:** Search "web designer for [your industry] Canada" or "web design agency [your industry]." You may find designers who specialize in your sector and market directly to it.

**In their portfolio:** When evaluating any designer, filter their portfolio for industry relevance. Have they built sites for businesses like yours? Do those sites address similar customer needs and conversion goals?

**In their intake questions:** A designer who asks sharp, specific questions about your customers in the first conversation likely has experience with businesses like yours. Vague or generic questions suggest they're treating your project as interchangeable with any other.

**In testimonials:** Look for client testimonials from businesses in your industry. Not just "they were great to work with" but "they understood exactly what our [trade / clinic / retail] customers needed."

### What to Ask About Industry Experience

- "Have you built websites for businesses in my industry before?"
- "What are the most common conversion goals for sites like mine?"
- "What trust signals matter most to [my industry's] customers?"
- "Are there sites you've built in this space that I could look at?"

If they can answer these confidently and specifically, they have relevant experience. If they hedge or deflect, they're figuring it out on your project.

### When Industry Experience Is Less Critical

There are cases where specific industry knowledge matters less:

- Your business is genuinely unique and doesn't fit a category a designer would specialize in
- The designer's process is strong enough that they'll learn your industry quickly through a thorough discovery process
- You're bringing deep industry knowledge yourself and need the designer mostly for execution

A great designer with a strong discovery process and genuine curiosity can build excellent sites for industries they haven't worked in before. Industry experience is a plus — it's not the only factor.

### The Alternative: Educate Your Designer Well

If you can't find someone with specific industry experience, invest heavily in the discovery phase. Provide:

- Examples of competitor sites you respect
- Examples of sites outside your industry that capture the feel you want
- A clear description of your customers and what moves them to action
- Testimonials and case studies from your best clients

A talented designer given rich context can get close to the quality of someone with direct industry experience.
