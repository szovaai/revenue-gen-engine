## How to Find a Web Designer Through LinkedIn

LinkedIn isn't the first place most people think of when they're looking for a web designer — but it probably should be. It's where serious professionals live, where you can verify someone's background, and where a cold message actually gets read.

Here's how to use LinkedIn effectively to find a web designer for your small business.

### Why LinkedIn Works Well for This

Other platforms (Fiverr, Upwork, even Google search) give you a polished front — a portfolio carefully curated to impress. LinkedIn gives you something closer to a professional record: where they've worked, how long, what clients they've served, and how they talk about their craft.

It also gives you social proof in a form that's harder to fake — mutual connections, endorsements from people in your network, and recommendations from real clients.

### Step 1: Use LinkedIn's Search Effectively

Start with the search bar. Type "web designer" and immediately filter by:

- **Location:** Select Canada, or your specific province or city
- **Industry:** "Design" or "Marketing and Advertising" or "Information Technology"
- **Connections:** Second-degree connections are particularly valuable — people who are connected to people you already know

You can also search for companies rather than individuals. Try "web design agency [city]" to find boutique agencies with a LinkedIn company page.

### Step 2: Evaluate Profiles Like a Pro

When you find candidates, here's what to assess:

**Their headline and summary.** Do they specifically mention small business experience? Do they clearly articulate what they do and who they help?

**Their portfolio links.** Most designers link to their website or portfolio directly in their profile. Follow those links and evaluate the work properly.

**Their work history.** How long have they been doing web design? Have they worked with businesses in your industry? Gaps in history or a very short tenure at multiple places can be worth asking about.

**Recommendations.** Read the written recommendations carefully. They're harder to fake than endorsements. Look for specifics — did the person mention deliverables, timelines, communication? Vague praise means less than specific examples.

**Endorsements.** Less reliable than recommendations, but a high number of skill endorsements from genuine-looking profiles adds weight.

### Step 3: Check Their Activity

What does the designer post about on LinkedIn? Designers who share their work, discuss their process, or engage thoughtfully with clients and peers are demonstrating both expertise and professionalism. Someone with no activity at all isn't disqualified — but someone actively sharing is showing you something real.

### Step 4: Reach Out Thoughtfully

A cold message on LinkedIn is actually welcomed when it's respectful and clear. Here's a template that works:

---

*Hi [Name],*

*I'm [your name], and I run [brief description of your business]. I'm looking for a web designer to build a professional website for my business, and your background caught my attention.*

*I'd love to connect and hear more about your work. Would you be open to a quick 15-minute call this week?*

*Thanks,*
*[Your name]*

---

Short, direct, respectful of their time. Don't write an essay. Don't open with flattery. Just say who you are, what you need, and ask for a small commitment (a short call, not an immediate proposal).

### Step 5: Vet Before You Commit

Once you're talking to a designer you found through LinkedIn, apply the same vetting you would with anyone else:

- Ask for live portfolio links
- Confirm their process and timeline
- Get a written proposal with clear scope
- Check references

LinkedIn gets you to the door. The real evaluation still happens in the conversation.
