## How to Avoid Getting Ripped Off by Web Designers

Most web designers are honest professionals doing legitimate work. But the industry has more than its share of bad actors — people who take deposits and disappear, deliver recycled templates at custom prices, or lock you into platforms they control so you can never leave.

Here's how to protect yourself at every stage.

### Before You Hire: Red Flags That Signal a Bad Actor

**They can't show you verifiable work.** Screenshots and mockups can be stolen. If a designer can't provide live website URLs you can visit and test, treat their portfolio with significant skepticism.

**Their own website is poor quality.** A web designer whose own site is slow, broken on mobile, or looks unprofessional is telling you something about their capabilities and standards.

**They pressure you to decide quickly.** "This price is only available today" or "I have another client ready to take this slot" are high-pressure sales tactics with no place in a professional service relationship. Legitimate designers don't need to pressure you.

**They ask for full payment upfront.** Standard practice is a deposit (50%) to begin, with the rest on completion. Full payment upfront removes all your leverage if things go wrong.

**They're vague about what's included.** A legitimate professional can tell you exactly what's in the scope. Vagueness is how scope gets gamed later with surprise charges.

**They promise what they can't deliver.** "Guaranteed first page of Google in 30 days." "Complete custom site in 48 hours." "Unlimited revisions forever." These aren't real promises — they're sales language that signals someone willing to say anything to close the deal.

### During the Project: How to Protect Yourself

**Get everything in a written contract.** This is non-negotiable. The contract should specify scope, timeline, payment terms, revision limits, what happens if either party needs to cancel, and who owns the final site.

**Don't pay in full before launch.** Hold back the final payment until the site is live and confirmed working. This is standard — any professional will agree to it.

**Own your own domain.** Your domain (yourcompany.ca) should be registered in your name, in your account. Never let a designer register your domain in their own name — if the relationship ends badly, you can lose your web address.

**Own your own hosting account.** Same principle. Your website should live in a hosting account that belongs to you, that you can access independently of the designer.

**Get your logins.** At the end of the project, you should receive every login credential for your site — hosting, domain, CMS, email, analytics. A designer who resists providing these is a designer who wants to maintain leverage over you.

**Don't approve work you're not happy with.** If something doesn't meet the agreed standard, say so clearly and document it in writing (email is fine). Don't be pressured into approving stages you're not satisfied with.

### After the Project: Common Post-Launch Issues

**The site breaks and they won't fix it.** Clarify post-launch support terms before you launch. What's covered? For how long? At what cost? Get it in writing.

**They claim to own the design.** Unless your contract says otherwise, you own what you paid for. If a designer claims ownership of the site or design after completion, you need to review your contract carefully and potentially seek legal advice.

**They raise prices to "maintain" your site.** If a designer has built your site on a proprietary system they control — rather than a standard CMS you can manage independently — they have leverage to charge whatever they want for ongoing access. Always ask upfront: "Is this built on a platform I can move to another host or manage independently?"

**They disappear.** It happens. If a designer goes silent mid-project and isn't responding to messages, escalate immediately. Send a formal written notice (email with read receipt) documenting the situation. If the project is on a platform, take screenshots of everything.

### The Single Best Protection: Hire Someone With a Verifiable Track Record

The most reliable protection against being ripped off is working with someone who has been doing this professionally for several years, has verifiable reviews, has real clients who can speak to their experience, and operates with a formal contract.

This isn't foolproof — but it removes the most common risk factors in one step.

### The Second-Best Protection: Trust Your Gut

If something feels off — if the communication is evasive, if the pricing seems designed to confuse, if you feel rushed or pressured — listen to that instinct. The right professional should make you feel informed and confident, not uncertain and pressured.

Walk away if you're not comfortable. There are enough good designers out there that you don't have to work with anyone who gives you a bad feeling.
