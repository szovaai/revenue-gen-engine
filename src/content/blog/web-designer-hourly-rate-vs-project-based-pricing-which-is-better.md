## Web Designer Hourly Rate vs. Project-Based Pricing: Which Is Better?

When you hire a web designer, one of the first things you'll encounter is a choice of billing model: pay by the hour, or pay a flat rate for the whole project. Both have their place, but for most small businesses, one is clearly better.

Here's how each model works, what the tradeoffs are, and how to decide.

### How Hourly Billing Works

The designer tracks the time they spend on your project and bills you at an agreed hourly rate. In Canada, professional web designers typically charge between **$75 and $175/hour**, with boutique agencies often sitting in the $100–$150 range.

**The appeal:** You only pay for the time actually worked. If the project turns out to be simpler than expected, you pay less.

**The reality for small businesses:** Hourly billing creates uncertainty that most small business owners find uncomfortable. You don't know what the final invoice will be until the project is done. Scope can grow as the project evolves. Hours can accumulate faster than expected. That uncertainty makes budgeting difficult — especially for a business watching cash flow carefully.

Hourly billing also creates a perverse incentive: the slower the designer works, the more they earn. Most designers are professional and don't abuse this — but the structure doesn't naturally reward efficiency.

### When Hourly Works Well

- **Ongoing maintenance and support:** If you need someone available for periodic updates, bug fixes, or content changes, hourly makes sense. You're paying for time, not a defined deliverable.
- **Small, undefined tasks:** "Fix this broken form" or "update these three photos" is easier to charge hourly than to project-price.
- **Established relationships:** When you already know the designer's work quality and speed, hourly billing becomes less risky.
- **Complex, evolving projects:** When the full scope genuinely can't be defined at the start, hourly protects the designer from scope creep while giving flexibility.

### How Project-Based Pricing Works

The designer quotes you a fixed price for a defined scope of work. You know from day one exactly what you're paying and what you're getting.

**The appeal:** Certainty. You can budget precisely. There are no surprises. The designer is incentivized to work efficiently because they earn the same regardless of how long it takes.

**The reality:** Project pricing only works when the scope is clearly defined upfront. If you keep changing your mind, adding pages, or shifting direction mid-project, you'll encounter change orders — additional charges for work outside the original scope.

Well-managed projects with clear briefs rarely hit significant change orders. Poorly scoped projects with vague requirements are where project pricing can get messy.

### Project Pricing Is Usually Better for a First Build

For a new small business website, project pricing is almost always the better choice. Here's why:

- You know your total cost before work begins
- You can compare quotes apples-to-apples across multiple designers
- The designer is motivated to stay on schedule
- Your budget process is clean

The only scenario where this breaks down is if you keep changing the scope. Which brings us to the most important advice in this article:

### Define Your Scope Clearly Before Signing Anything

Whether you choose hourly or project pricing, the most important thing you can do is define exactly what you want before the project begins. That means:

- The number of pages
- The features required
- Who provides the content
- How many revision rounds are included
- What the timeline is

Unclear scope leads to disputes under any billing model. Clear scope leads to successful projects.

### What to Ask About Billing Before You Hire

- "Do you offer project-based pricing or hourly?"
- "If project-based, what causes the price to change from the quoted amount?"
- "If hourly, can you give me an estimate range for this project?"
- "How are change requests handled?"
