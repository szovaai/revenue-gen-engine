## When DIY Might Be Enough

There are real scenarios where building your own website with a platform like Squarespace, Wix, or Shopify makes sense:

**You're testing a business idea.** If you're not sure the business will stick, a $30/month Squarespace site is a smart way to establish an online presence while you validate the concept. Once you're generating revenue, upgrade.

**You have a very simple need.** A one-page "digital business card" with your contact info, a brief description, and links to your social media? You can probably do that yourself without much pain.

**You have genuine design skills.** Some business owners have a background in design or marketing. If you can look at a site and know intuitively why it does or doesn't work, you might be able to produce something decent.

**Your budget is genuinely $0.** If you have absolutely no money to spend, a mediocre DIY site is better than no site. But as soon as revenue allows, invest in doing it properly.

## When You Need a Professional

Here's where most small business owners genuinely need professional help:

**When your website is your storefront.** If a potential customer's first impression of your business comes through your website — and for most businesses in 2024, it does — that impression matters enormously. A site that looks amateur makes your business look amateur. It doesn't matter how good your actual product or service is.

**When you need to rank on Google.** DIY platforms are decent, but getting found in search requires proper SEO setup, site structure, page speed optimization, and strategic content. Most business owners building their own site don't have the knowledge to do this well.

**When you need the site to convert.** Looking decent is not the same as converting visitors into customers. A professional web designer understands how to guide users toward a specific action — a call, a booking, a purchase. DIY builders give you the canvas; they don't teach you conversion strategy.

**When your time has significant value.** The real cost of a DIY website isn't the platform fee — it's your time. 20–40 hours of fumbling with templates, troubleshooting broken forms, and trying to figure out why your mobile menu doesn't work is 20–40 hours away from your actual business.

**When you need it done right the first time.** Many businesses go through the DIY phase, produce something they're not happy with, and then pay a professional to rebuild it anyway. They've paid twice — once in time, once in money.

## The Real Question to Ask

It's not "can I build a website myself?" — you probably can. The real question is: "Is this the best use of my time, and will the result actually serve my business?"

For most established small businesses, the answer to both parts of that question points clearly toward hiring a professional.

## The Middle Ground: Hybrid Approach

Some businesses take a middle path — they hire a professional to design and launch the site, then take over maintenance themselves using a content management system (CMS) that doesn't require coding.

This is often the smartest approach. You get professional quality without permanent reliance on an agency for every minor change.
