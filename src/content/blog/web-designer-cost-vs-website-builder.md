On the surface, the math seems obvious: $30/month for Squarespace beats $1,500 for a designer every time. Right?

Not quite. When you do the full accounting — including your time, opportunity cost, and the quality of the result — the comparison gets a lot more interesting.

## The True Cost of a DIY Website Builder

Let's use Squarespace as an example. The Business plan runs about $33 CAD/month, or roughly $400/year. That sounds very affordable.

But here's what that number doesn't include:

**Your time.** How long will it take you to build the site? For someone who's never done it, 20–40 hours is a realistic estimate just to get something that looks acceptable. That's not counting learning the platform, troubleshooting issues, or future updates. What is an hour of your time worth? At a modest $50/hour, 30 hours of your time = $1,500.

**Additional tools you'll need.** Stock photos, form plugins, SEO tools, analytics — many of these are either not included in the base plan or require paid add-ons.

**The ongoing subscription.** At $400/year, a five-year cost is $2,000 — plus all price increases over time.

**The quality gap.** Unless you have a background in design, your DIY site will likely look it. That has a real cost in credibility, conversions, and customer confidence that's hard to quantify but very real.

**Rebuilding costs.** Many small businesses go the DIY route, aren't happy with the result, and eventually pay a professional to rebuild it anyway. They pay twice.

## The Hidden Variable: Conversion

This is where the DIY vs. professional comparison decisively shifts.

Say 100 people visit your website in a month. A professionally designed site might convert 3–5% into leads or sales. A DIY site might convert 1–2%.

If your average customer is worth $500, the difference between a 2% and 4% conversion rate on 100 monthly visitors is:

- **DIY:** 2 customers × $500 = $1,000/month
- **Professional:** 4 customers × $500 = $2,000/month

That's $1,000/month more in revenue — $12,000/year — attributable to a better website. The cost difference between DIY and professional becomes negligible almost immediately.

## When Website Builders Make Sense

To be fair, there are scenarios where a website builder is the right tool:

- You're testing a business idea before committing
- You have a genuine design background and the time to use it
- Your needs are very simple (single-page, minimal content)
- You're in the very early stages with truly no budget

For established businesses or those ready to grow, though, the math and the quality argument both favour a professional build.
