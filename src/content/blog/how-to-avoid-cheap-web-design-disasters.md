## How to Avoid Cheap Web Design Disasters (Lessons From the Trenches)

The story is painfully common. A small business owner finds someone offering a full website for $250. It sounds too good to be true, but the portfolio looks decent, so they go ahead. Three months later, they have a half-finished site, a designer who's gone quiet, and no working website to show for it.

Or: they do get a finished site — but it looks like it was built in 2011, ranks nowhere on Google, breaks on mobile, and the contact form has never once submitted successfully.

Cheap web design disasters are avoidable. Here's how.

### Understand Why Cheap Is Risky

Rock-bottom pricing in web design signals one of the following:

- **Inexperience:** Someone learning on your project, with all the mistakes that implies.
- **Offshoring to the lowest-cost labour:** Quality becomes highly variable and accountability nearly disappears.
- **Templates with near-zero customization:** You're paying for someone to change the colours and swap the photos — not to build you something.
- **No plan for your success:** A $200 website is a transaction. A good designer at a fair price is an investment.

None of these automatically produce bad results — but the odds are stacked against you.

### The Signs You're About to Make a Mistake

Before you hand over a deposit, watch for these warning patterns:

**They can't show you live sites.** Only screenshots, mockups, or Behance links. Real sites can be tested. Screenshots can be borrowed from anyone.

**Their own website is poor quality.** If a web designer's own site is slow, broken on mobile, or looks unprofessional, why would yours be different?

**Vague communication from the start.** Short responses, slow replies, no follow-up questions about your business. This is a preview of how the project will be managed.

**No formal proposal or contract.** "We'll sort the details as we go" is how disputes happen. Everything should be in writing before work begins.

**Price that assumes very little work.** A complete professional website takes 20–60+ hours of real work. A price point that only makes sense if someone is being paid $5/hour should prompt hard questions.

**Promises that sound too good.** "SEO guaranteed." "First page of Google in 30 days." "Fully custom design in 48 hours." These aren't achievable claims from a legitimate professional.

### What Happens When It Goes Wrong

The cost of a cheap web design disaster isn't just the wasted initial payment. Consider the full picture:

- **Lost time:** Weeks or months waiting on a designer who keeps promising "almost done"
- **Lost customers:** Every week you don't have a credible website is a week potential customers are going elsewhere
- **Recovery costs:** Hiring a second designer to fix or rebuild what the first one left behind
- **Reputational damage:** If a bad site does go live before you notice the problems, it's been representing your business — poorly

The business that spends $250 on a disaster and then $1,500 to fix it has spent $1,750. The business that spent $1,200 on a professional first time is ahead.

### How to Protect Yourself

**Set a realistic budget.** Know what a professional build actually costs (in Canada, $997–$2,500 for a quality small business site) and budget accordingly. This calibrates your expectations from the start.

**Require a written contract.** Scope, timeline, payment terms, revision limits, ownership of the final site — everything in writing.

**Pay in stages.** A deposit (typically 50%) to start, with the remainder on completion. Never pay 100% upfront.

**Check references.** Ask for one or two past clients you can actually contact. Don't just read testimonials on the designer's site.

**Test the portfolio.** Click through live sites. Do they load fast? Do they work on your phone? Does the contact form actually submit?

**Trust your gut.** If the early communication feels off, if something doesn't add up, if you feel like you're being rushed — listen to that instinct.
