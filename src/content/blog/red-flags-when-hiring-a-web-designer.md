## Red Flags When Hiring a Web Designer (What to Watch For Before You Pay a Cent)

Most bad web design experiences are preventable. The warning signs are almost always there — they just get overlooked in the excitement of starting a new project or the pressure of needing a site quickly.

Here are the red flags that should make you slow down, ask hard questions, or walk away entirely.

### Red Flag #1: No Portfolio — or a Portfolio With No Live Links

If a designer can't show you real, functioning websites they've built, that's a serious problem. Screenshots alone can be faked or borrowed. Mockups look nothing like live sites. Anyone claiming professional experience should have live URLs to show you.

No portfolio at all means one of two things: they're brand new (which doesn't disqualify them, but should inform your expectations) or they have something to hide.

### Red Flag #2: Pricing That Seems Too Low to Be Real

A complete professional website for $150 is not a deal — it's either stolen work, a template with your logo slapped on it, or a project that will never actually launch.

Legitimate designers charge what they do because web design takes real time and real skill. Prices that seem impossibly low should raise immediate questions about what you're actually getting.

### Red Flag #3: Asking for Full Payment Upfront

Standard practice in web design is a deposit (typically 50%) to begin, with the remainder due on completion or at a defined milestone. Asking for 100% payment before any work has been delivered removes your leverage entirely if something goes wrong.

There are exceptions — very small projects or established ongoing relationships — but for a new engagement, this is a major flag.

### Red Flag #4: Vague Scope and No Written Contract

If a designer won't put the project scope in writing — what's being built, how many pages, what's included, what the timeline is — run. A handshake agreement might feel casual and friendly, but it leaves you completely unprotected if the project goes sideways.

Any professional will have a contract or a formal project proposal. If they resist putting things in writing, that tells you everything.

### Red Flag #5: They Don't Ask About Your Business

A designer who launches straight into telling you what you'll get without asking what your business does, who your customers are, and what you want the site to accomplish is building something for themselves, not for you.

Good web design is driven by understanding the business, the audience, and the goal. A designer who doesn't ask those questions will produce something that looks fine in a screenshot and fails in real life.

### Red Flag #6: Overpromising on Timelines

"I can have a full website ready in 48 hours!" is almost never true for a legitimate, professional build. It usually means a template with minimal customization, low-quality copy, and no time for proper testing.

A professional small business website takes 2–4 weeks to do properly. Anyone promising significantly less should be pressed on exactly what shortcuts they're planning to take.

### Red Flag #7: Overpromising on SEO Results

"We guarantee you'll be on page one of Google within 30 days!" is either a lie or a sign they're planning to use black-hat SEO tactics that will get your site penalized.

No one can guarantee Google rankings. A good designer will set up the technical foundation for SEO — clean structure, fast load times, proper metadata — but organic rankings take time, content, and ongoing effort.

### Red Flag #8: Poor Communication From the Start

How a designer communicates before they have your money is the best preview of how they'll communicate during the project. If they take days to reply to an initial inquiry, send unclear or unprofessional messages, or fail to follow up after a call — assume that pattern continues.

Slow, unclear communication mid-project causes delays, misunderstandings, and frustration.

### Red Flag #9: No Post-Launch Support Plan

What happens when your site breaks six months after launch? What happens when you need to update a page and can't figure out how?

A designer who has no answer to these questions — or says "that's not my problem" — is leaving you stranded. Even if you plan to manage the site yourself, a professional should walk you through how to do that before handing over the keys.

### Red Flag #10: You Can't Find Any Trace of Them Online

A legitimate web design business should have at minimum: a professional website, a Google Business Profile, and some form of verifiable online presence. If the only way you found them was a Facebook post and you can't verify anything about them — be very cautious.

### Trust Your Gut

Beyond the specific flags above, trust your instincts. If a designer makes you feel rushed, dismissive of your questions, or like you're being sold rather than served — that discomfort is usually telling you something.

The best designers make you feel heard, informed, and confident. If that's not happening in the conversation before you've paid anything, it won't magically happen after.
