## Where Do Most Small Businesses Find Web Designers?

If you're trying to figure out where to start your search, it helps to know where other business owners have actually found designers — and more importantly, where they've found good ones. Here's a practical look at the most common sources, and what the experience is typically like.

### Referrals From Other Business Owners

This is consistently the most reliable source. When a business owner recommends their web designer, they're vouching for the full experience — the process, the communication, the result, and the relationship afterward.

Referrals come from:
- Peer business networks (BIA groups, industry associations, Chamber of Commerce events)
- Direct conversations with other owners whose websites you admire
- Social media groups for small business owners in your area

If you can find a designer this way, start here. The built-in accountability of a referral changes the dynamic.

### Google Search

Most business owners start with Google, and it's a solid first step. Searching "web designer [city]" or "small business website design Canada" returns a manageable list of options to evaluate.

A useful meta-signal: a web designer who ranks on page one of Google for their own services has demonstrated they understand SEO — which is relevant since you'll likely want your own site to rank.

### LinkedIn

LinkedIn is underused for this purpose, but it's one of the better research tools available. You can find local or Canadian designers, verify their professional history, see mutual connections, and read client recommendations that are harder to fake than a testimonial on their own website.

It's particularly effective for finding boutique agencies and experienced independent designers who don't rely on the noise of gig platforms.

### Freelance Platforms (Fiverr, Upwork)

These are popular — and for simple, well-defined, lower-stakes projects, they can work. For a primary business website, the risks (described in detail in Article 15) are significant. Many small businesses have started here and ended up rebuilding their site professionally after a disappointing experience.

### Portfolio Sites (Dribbble, Behance)

Useful for finding designers with a particular visual style. Less useful for assessing whether that designer can build something that converts or whether they'll communicate reliably. Best used as a discovery step, followed by rigorous vetting before hiring.

### Social Media (Instagram, Facebook Groups)

Designers who showcase their work on Instagram can be found through industry hashtags or business-focused Facebook groups. The quality range here is even wider than on gig platforms, so extra vetting is required. That said, some excellent local designers build their business almost entirely through social.

### Their Own Website Showing Up in Search

Sometimes you find a designer the same way your future customers will find you — they've done the SEO work on their own site and show up when you search relevant terms. This is both a practical signal of their skills and a convenient discovery method.

### What the Best-Performing Businesses Have in Common

When small businesses report finding a web designer they'd recommend to others, a few patterns emerge:

- They found the designer through a referral or personal connection
- They vetted the designer thoroughly before committing — reviewing live sites, asking hard questions, checking references
- They chose based on fit and process quality, not just price
- They worked with a boutique agency or experienced independent designer, not a gig platform

The businesses that struggled most often cut corners at the sourcing or vetting stage.
