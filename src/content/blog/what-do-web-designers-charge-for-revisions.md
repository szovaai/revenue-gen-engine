## What Do Web Designers Charge for Revisions? (And How to Avoid Surprise Fees)

Revisions are where a lot of web design projects go sideways. What feels like a small change to you can mean hours of additional work for a designer. And if the revision policy wasn't clearly agreed upfront, you may be looking at fees you didn't budget for — or a strained relationship you didn't expect.

Here's how revision pricing typically works, and how to protect yourself.

### What "Revisions" Actually Means in Web Design

In web design contracts, "revisions" usually means changes to the design or content within the original agreed scope. This includes things like:

- Adjusting the layout or visual design of a page
- Changing colours, fonts, or imagery
- Updating the copy on a page
- Reordering elements or sections
- Modifying a form or button

What revisions typically do *not* cover:

- Adding pages that weren't in the original scope
- Adding features that weren't specified (booking systems, e-commerce, live chat)
- Redesigning sections after they've been approved
- Requesting fundamentally different design directions after sign-off

That last point trips people up most often. If you approved a design direction and then decide mid-build that you want a completely different look, that's generally not a revision — it's a change in scope.

### How Most Designers Structure Revisions

**Included rounds:** Most professional designers include one to three rounds of revisions in the project price. A "round" means you review the work, compile all your feedback in one go, and the designer implements those changes. One round of revisions, done properly, should address most adjustments.

**Per-round fees:** Additional revision rounds beyond what's included are typically charged at an hourly rate ($75–$175/hour in Canada) or a flat fee per round ($100–$300).

**Unlimited revisions:** Some designers advertise "unlimited revisions." Read the fine print carefully. This almost always means unlimited changes within the original scope — not unlimited changes to anything, including fundamental redirections.

### What You Pay for Revisions Outside Scope

Additions to scope — new pages, new features, significant redesigns — are typically quoted as separate line items when they arise. This is standard practice and not unreasonable. The risk is not knowing this in advance.

A typical out-of-scope rate: $100–$175/hour for an experienced designer or boutique agency.

### How to Minimize Revision Costs

**Consolidate your feedback.** Don't send revisions piecemeal. Review the full design once, gather all your notes, and send everything in one organized document. "Can you change the header, also the button colour, also I realized the About page needs a section added" is how rounds multiply.

**Be clear in your initial brief.** Most revisions are symptoms of a brief that wasn't specific enough. The more clearly you articulate what you want — visual references, specific copy, examples of sites you like and don't like — the closer the first draft will be to what you actually want.

**Get stakeholder alignment early.** If others need to approve the site (a partner, a manager, a spouse), involve them in the brief stage, not the revision stage. Surprise opinions late in the project cause the most expensive revisions.

**Approve stages sequentially.** Most designers present work in stages — wireframes, then design, then development. Approve each stage before the next begins, rather than reviewing everything at the end. Changes are cheaper and faster earlier in the process.

### What to Ask Before You Sign

- "How many revision rounds are included?"
- "What's your definition of a revision vs. a scope change?"
- "What's your hourly rate for work outside the original scope?"
- "How do you handle feedback — do you prefer it in writing?"

Getting these answers upfront eliminates almost all revision-related surprises.
