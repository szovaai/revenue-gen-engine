## How to Interview a Web Designer (A Practical Guide for Small Business Owners)

Choosing a web designer based only on price and a portfolio is like hiring an employee without ever having a conversation. The interview is where you find out whether someone truly understands your business — and whether you can work with them.

Here's how to run a web designer interview that actually tells you something useful.

### Set the Right Expectations Going In

An interview with a web designer isn't an interrogation. It's a conversation to assess fit — on both sides. A great designer will also be interviewing you: asking about your business, your goals, your customers, and your timeline. If they're just pitching and not asking questions, that tells you something.

Come prepared. Have a basic idea of what you want — how many pages, what the site needs to accomplish, your rough budget, and your timeline. You don't need technical knowledge. You need business clarity.

### The Opening Question

Start here: **"Can you walk me through how you approach a new project from discovery to launch?"**

This question reveals their process, their priorities, and how organized they are. Listen for:

- Do they start with strategy and business understanding, or jump straight to design?
- Is the process clear and repeatable, or vague and ad hoc?
- Where do they involve the client, and where do they work independently?
- What do they need from you to do their best work?

A designer with a strong process will answer this with confidence and clarity. A designer without one will waffle.

### Questions to Ask About Your Specific Project

- "Have you built sites for businesses like mine? Can you show me examples?"
- "What platform would you recommend for my needs, and why?"
- "What content do you need from me, and when?"
- "What does your revision process look like?"
- "How do you handle it if the project goes over timeline?"

### Questions About Their Business

- "How long have you been doing this professionally?"
- "Is it just you, or do you have a team?"
- "How many projects do you take on at a time?"

That last question matters more than it sounds. A designer juggling 20 projects simultaneously will give yours less attention than one with a focused, manageable client load.

### Questions About Money and Contracts

- "What does your pricing structure look like for a project like mine?"
- "What are your payment terms?"
- "What typically causes a project to go over budget?"
- "Do you use a written contract?"

If they bristle at the contract question, that's a problem. Contracts protect both parties. Any professional will expect to use one.

### The Question That Separates Good From Great

Ask this: **"If this site goes live and isn't generating the results we hoped for, what do you think the most likely reasons would be?"**

This question does two things. First, it shows whether they understand that a website's success depends on more than just design — content quality, traffic, conversion strategy, and your offer all matter. Second, it shows whether they're confident enough to be honest with you.

A great answer might look like: "The design and build will be solid — but if your copy isn't compelling, or if you're not driving traffic to the site, the results will be limited. I can help with the design; I can advise on copy; but I want to make sure you have realistic expectations going in."

That's a designer who cares about your actual outcome, not just the deliverable.

### What to Listen For (Beyond the Words)

Pay attention to:

- **Response time.** How quickly did they reply to your initial inquiry? How quickly did they confirm the interview time?
- **Preparation.** Did they look at your current site (if you have one) or research your business before the call?
- **Listening.** Do they let you finish your thoughts, or do they cut in with their pitch?
- **Honesty.** Do they tell you what you want to hear, or do they push back thoughtfully where they see a problem?

### After the Interview

Compare candidates on process, communication quality, relevant experience, and gut feeling — not just price. The cheapest option rarely gives you the best outcome.

If a designer made you feel heard, gave you clear and honest answers, and seemed genuinely interested in your business — that's worth paying a fair price for.
