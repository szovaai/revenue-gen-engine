Finding a good web designer feels harder than it should be. There's no shortage of people claiming to do web design — but a great one who understands small business, communicates clearly, and delivers on time? That takes a little more work to find.

Here are the best places to look, and what to watch for at each one.

## 1. Google Search (Local + Service Terms)

Start with what your customers would do — search Google. Try terms like "web designer for small business in [your city]" or "small business website design Canada." The agencies and designers that show up at the top have already proven they know how to get found online, which is a reasonable signal that they know what they're doing.

Check their website, their portfolio, and look for case studies or client results — not just pretty screenshots.

## 2. Referrals From Other Business Owners

Ask around. If you know another small business owner with a website you admire, just ask who built it and whether they'd recommend them. Word-of-mouth referrals are still the most reliable way to find someone trustworthy, because they come with a real human vouching for the experience.

## 3. LinkedIn

LinkedIn is underrated for finding web designers, especially in Canada. Search for "web designer" or "web design agency" and filter by location. You can review their profile, see their endorsements, check their portfolio links, and often message them directly.

This approach tends to surface more professional, established designers compared to gig platforms.

## 4. Freelance Platforms (Upwork, Fiverr)

These are options, but use them carefully. You'll find talented designers here — but you'll also find people who overpromise and underdeliver. The anonymity of gig platforms can make accountability difficult if something goes wrong.

If you go this route, look for designers with long track records, specific small business experience, and verifiable reviews — not just star ratings.

## 5. Portfolio Sites (Dribbble, Behance)

Dribbble and Behance are where designers showcase their best work. If you have a strong sense of the visual style you want, these platforms let you search by aesthetic and reach out directly to designers whose work appeals to you.

Keep in mind: Dribbble and Behance show creative work, not necessarily conversion-focused design. A portfolio that looks stunning isn't automatically one that will drive business results.

## 6. Industry-Specific Directories

Some industries have curated directories of service providers. Check if your sector (trades, retail, hospitality, health, etc.) has a business association with a preferred vendor list.

## 7. Local Business Groups and Chambers of Commerce

Your local Chamber of Commerce or business improvement association often has member directories. Finding a designer through your local business community adds a layer of accountability — they have a local reputation to protect.

## What to Look For Once You Find Someone

Wherever you look, assess candidates on:

- **Portfolio quality:** Does their past work actually look good? Does it look modern?
- **Relevant experience:** Have they worked with businesses like yours?
- **Communication:** Do they respond promptly? Do they ask smart questions?
- **Process clarity:** Can they clearly explain how the project will work?
- **Pricing transparency:** Are they upfront about costs, or vague about "it depends"?

## Why "Cheapest Available" Isn't a Strategy

It's tempting to optimize for price when you're searching. But web design is one of those services where the cheapest option tends to cost you more in the long run — through revisions, emergency fixes, rebuilds, and lost business from a site that doesn't convert.

You're looking for value, not just price. Value means a site that looks professional, loads fast, works on mobile, and actually helps your business grow.
